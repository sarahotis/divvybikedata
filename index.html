Introduction¶

If you liked biking, which would you rather: a bike of your own, or a bikeshare membership? A bike, which requires maintenance and protection from theft or rust, gives you the freedom to go for long rides, park wherever you like, and leave the city for some scenic trails. But, it's not easily transportable to other cities, and you may not be able to afford to buy a lasting bike or have space for one. What would push you to join a bikeshare program? If nothing would, would you still use bikeshares as a way to get around once or twice a year for $3?

In 2015, Chicago's bikeshare program Divvy, began serving the largest area in North America. They updated their pricing plans, too, and began to advertise to low-income riders. By 2015, Divvybikes were only two years old. Divvy has released its trip data from 2013 through today, and we are invited to find out more about the history of this bikeshare program. What can we learn from Divvy's 2015 data?

Ready? Let's prepare for our journey!

Eliminate outliers in user data¶

The gender and birthyear atrributes are giving us the most trouble, and Divvybike offers no race, income, or lifestyle data. Still, if gender and birthyear enter our analysis later, we will thank ourselves for having tidied the data now.

According to the US Census Bureau, approximately 1.4% of the population each year since 2015 has been 85 or older. We can safely discount all these ages without significantly affecting our birthyear statistics for bikeshare riders. More statistically speaking, we can discount all ages that are beyond the "99.7%" of data that the 3-sigma rule describes. Below, we set outliers of our lower bound, which are below mean - 3*SD, to null.

Standardizing datetime columns¶

We are aggregating lots of trip data and combining multiple datasets, so units should reflect standard formatting. People like to be creative with datetime formats, so let us write a method to standardize datetime to ISO 8601 extended formats for any column. We will standardize the chicago_bikeshare_data immediately. We will also add in an hourly datetime column for convenience merging hourly weather data.

In [9]:
DATETIME_MINUTES_INDEX = 14

def datetime_standard(dtime):
    prog = re.compile(r'^2015-')
    result = prog.match(dtime)
    if result:
        dtime = datetime.datetime.strptime(dtime,'%Y-%m-%d %H:%M:%S')
    else:
        dtime = datetime.datetime.strptime(dtime,'%m/%d/%Y %H:%M')
    return dtime.strftime('%Y-%m-%d %H:%M:%S')

# Re-formatting datetime columns
chicago_bikeshare_data['starttime'] = [datetime_standard(d) for d in chicago_bikeshare_data.starttime]
chicago_bikeshare_data['stoptime'] = [datetime_standard(d) for d in chicago_bikeshare_data.stoptime]
chicago_bikeshare_data['datetime'] = [(d[:DATETIME_MINUTES_INDEX] + '00:00') for d in chicago_bikeshare_data.starttime]

# Adding weather classification for safety (categorical) and risk  #
# index (numerical representation) to dataset for easier analysis: #
def classify_weather(wtype):
    safe = ['broken clouds', 'few clouds', 'overcast clouds', 'proximity thunderstorm', 'scattered clouds',
                'sky is clear']
    mediocre = ['drizzle', 'fog', 'haze', 'light intensity drizzle', 'light snow', 'mist', 
                'proximity shower rain', 'proximity thunderstorm with drizzle', 'smoke',
                'thunderstorm with light drizzle']
    dangerous = ['dust', 'freezing rain', 'heavy intensity drizzle', 'heavy intensity rain', 'heavy snow',
                'light rain', 'moderate rain', 'proximity thunderstorm with rain', 'snow', 'squalls',
                'thunderstorm with heavy rain', 'thunderstorm with rain','thunderstorm with light rain',
                'very heavy rain']
    return 'safe' if wtype in safe else ('mediocre' if wtype in mediocre else 'dangerous')

def risk_index(safety):
    if safety == "safe":
        return 0
    elif safety == "mediocre":
        return .5
    elif safety == "dangerous":
        return 1
    else:
        return None

# Adding columns of interest to weather dataframe

weather2015['risk_index'] = [(risk_index(item)) for item in weather2015.safety]

Destination #3: Exploratory Data Analysis¶

Until now, we've been on a straight path. Collect the data, tidy the data, and now... do something with the data? Let us take some detours to explore which street we'd like to take next with our data! We want to arrive at a meaningful destination, so we try to gain as much as possible from our data before pedaling more through our data lifecycle. Let us explore basic information about our data.

We can ask so many questions about our data. What can we learn about its inventory, about popular trip times, about users, about geolocations? For now, let us stick to one theme: distance.

Distance in our dataset may be studied from a variety of perspectives:

  1. How far, on average, do people travel each day?
  2. Do distances peak at different times of the day? Can we predict any trends about distance based on time of day?
  3. Do people of different ages travel different distances?
  4. Do a higher percentage of subscribers or customers travel longer distances?
  5. How does weather affect distance? If weather affects distance, does this vary by time of day, usertype, or season?
  6. Is there any way we can track bikes to see whether bikes that are used a certain number of times begin to be used less? Or, for shorter distances? Or, during bad weather they are used for shorter distances?
  7. Are there certain locations from which riders take longer trips? Tangentially, can we learn anything about popular places in Chicago based on this distance data?

We will attempt to answer the first of these questions together. In the future, we look at certain other surface-level aspects of our dataset, and then pedal into a deeper exploration of our data.

Questions to consider about bikesharing data¶

  • Do riders go the same distances at different speeds, on average, based on different peak times of the day?
  • Does elevation in Chicago affect the direction of travel? Is there a higher demand for and a shortage of bikes at elevated docks, and a lower demand and surplus of bikes at lower elevation docks? (Who are we kidding? This is the flat Midwest.)
  • Why so many men in their 30s? What is their race and their occupation? What motivates them over other demographics to choose the Divvybike commute?
  • Does weather affect demand for rides? Specifically, can we compare categorical precipitation and weather data to bike demand for any meaningful results?
  • Are there less rides during and after bad weather, or just during bad weather? (How tough are Chicagoans?) How fast do bikeshare riders return to the streets?
  • What kinds of inventory analysis can be done on this dataset? What insight and policy decisions might come of that?
  • Are the rides that occur during bad weather shorter, on average?
  • (Bonus:) Is there a way to tell how much tourists contribute to the user base? If so, during what seasons, and since there are so many other modes of transportation--subway, Metra, water taxi, Uber, walking, etc.--where do they mostly prefer to ride with their bikes?
  • At what times of the day is bike demand the highest; when is bike demand at its lowest?
  • Is time of day a good predictor for trip duration?
  • How accurate were Divvybike's predictions of station popularity (presumably) when they decided upon dock capacities? Is dock capacity a good predictor for bikeshare activity at each station?

Feel free to explore questions that draw your attention, and please share your results!
