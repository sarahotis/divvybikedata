Introduction¶

Questions for to consider about bikesharing data¶

  • Do riders go the same distances at different speeds, on average, based on different peak times of the day?
  • Does elevation in Chicago affect the direction of travel? Is there a higher demand for and a shortage of bikes at elevated docks, and a lower demand and surplus of bikes at lower elevation docks? (Who are we kidding? This is the flat Midwest.)
  • Why so many men in their 30s? What is their race and their occupation? What motivates them over other demographics to choose the Divvybike commute?
  • Does weather affect demand for rides? Specifically, can we compare categorical precipitation and weather data to bike demand for any meaningful results?
  • Are there less rides during and after bad weather, or just during bad weather? (How tough are Chicagoans?)
  • Are the rides that occur during bad weather shorter, on average?
  • (Bonus:) Is there a way to tell how much tourists contribute to the user base? If so, during what seasons, and since there are so many other modes of transportation--subway, Metra, water taxi, Uber, walking, etc.--where do they mostly prefer to ride with their bikes?
  • At what times of the day is bike demand the highest; when is bike demand at its lowest?
  • Is time of day a good predictor for trip duration?
  • How accurate were Divvybike's predictions of station popularity (presumably) when they decided upon dock capacities? Is dock capacity a good predictor for bikeshare activity at each station?

We will not answer most of these questions. Feel free to explore questions that draw your attention, and please share your results! :)

Our dataset comes with a row for each Divvybike trip. Each row contains trip id, bike id, from- and to- station ids and names, start and stop times in datetime format, trip durations, and user data like whether they are a subscriber (=have an annual membership) or whether they are a customer (=are a casual user without a membership), their gender, and their birthyear. It is worth noting that tripdurations are measured in seconds.

We can see that our gigantic dataset (with 12 columns and over 3 million rows) has some missing values. We will want to tidy up the data soon by removing columns with null or entirely nonsensical entries, changing column names and converting data types where convenient, and keeping only the data that we want.

Extracting time information from starttime and appending to dataset¶

This will make trends more clear, as patterns will likely differ on the weekends.

In [9]:
weekday_id = []
weekday = []
week = []
hour = []
workweek = []
    
for i in range(len(chicago_bikeshare_data)):
    dtime = chicago_bikeshare_data.iloc[i]['starttime']
    prog = re.compile(r'^2015')
    result = prog.match(dtime)
    if result:
        time_attrs = datetime.datetime.strptime(dtime,'%Y-%m-%d %H:%M:%S') # Dates are recorded in multiple formats
    else:
        time_attrs = datetime.datetime.strptime(dtime,'%m/%d/%Y %H:%M')
    weekdays = ['sunday','monday','tuesday','wednesday','thursday','friday','saturday']
    wkdy_id = (time_attrs.timetuple().tm_wday + 1) % 7
    weekday_id.append(wkdy_id)
    weekday.append(weekdays[wkdy_id]) # 0 is Monday in documentation, so 1 is Sunday now
    week.append(time_attrs.isocalendar()[1])
    hour.append(time_attrs.timetuple().tm_hour)
    workweek.append('y' if (wkdy_id > 0 and wkdy_id < 6) else 'n')

# Add all the temp data from datetime format to main dataset through full join
temp = pd.DataFrame({'week': week, 'weekday': weekday,'weekday_id':weekday_id, 'hour':hour, 
                     'workweek': workweek, 'starttime': chicago_bikeshare_data['starttime']})
chicago_bikeshare_data = pd.merge(chicago_bikeshare_data, temp[['week', 'weekday','weekday_id', 'hour', 
                     'workweek', 'starttime']], on='starttime', how='left')

Let's take a look at Chicago's weather trends. What are the different types of weather that visit Chicago? We can peek at the different types with groupby('weather').groups.keys(). Then, we can make a dataframe to reflect the frequency of each weather type and assign relative safeties to different weather types for bikers. Once we have that dataframe, we will visualize the frequency of safe, mediocre, and dangerous weather in a pie chart. This will help us begin to explore our data.

# See notes on weather classification, below
def classify_weather(wtype):
    safe = ['broken clouds', 'few clouds', 'overcast clouds', 'proximity thunderstorm', 'scattered clouds',
                'sky is clear']
    mediocre = ['drizzle', 'fog', 'haze', 'light intensity drizzle', 'light snow', 'mist', 
                'proximity shower rain', 'proximity thunderstorm with drizzle', 'smoke',
                'thunderstorm with light drizzle']
    dangerous = ['dust', 'freezing rain', 'heavy intensity drizzle', 'heavy intensity rain', 'heavy snow',
                'light rain', 'moderate rain', 'proximity thunderstorm with rain', 'snow', 'squalls',
                'thunderstorm with heavy rain', 'thunderstorm with rain','thunderstorm with light rain',
                'very heavy rain']
    return 'safe' if wtype in safe else ('mediocre' if wtype in mediocre else 'dangerous')

# Pie chart for weather safety!
def make_autopct(values):
    def my_autopct(pct):
        total = sum(values)
        val = int(round(pct*total/100.0))
        return '{p:.2f}%  ({v:d})'.format(p=pct,v=val)
    return my_autopct

weather_safety = {}
for x in weather_freq.groupby('safety').groups.keys():
    weather_safety[x] = weather_freq[weather_freq['safety'] == x].sum().frequency
